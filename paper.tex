% Abstract

\begin{abstract}
We address the engineering of a computational architecture able to ingest and
synchronize heterogeneous information sources for daily directional trading
decisions: continuous price series, high-frequency unstructured text processed
through a financial language model, and discrete low-frequency corporate
filings. The central difficulty is not modelling each modality in isolation but
fusing them coherently under severe asymmetries in frequency, dimensionality and
availability. We propose a four-branch architecture---patch-based temporal encoder, FinBERT
attention-pooled news, decayed filing projection, per-day scalar
encoder---fused via a gated layer with availability masking. The architecture is validated on the FinMMEval
Task~3 corpus~\cite{qian2025agents} (twelve assets spanning equities and cryptocurrencies). With per-asset volatility-adaptive labelling, decoupled decision optimization,
and SWA, the validation-selected model attains \textbf{23.3\%} cumulative
return, a \textbf{4.21} Sharpe, and \textbf{4.45\%} max drawdown. We frame these as validation-selected operating points: across five seeds test
Sharpe varies widely ($\sigma \approx 1.9$). Two negative results---SWA does
not raise the across-seed mean, and ensembles degraded---suggest the binding
constraint is the experimental protocol, not model capacity.

\keywords{Multimodal fusion \and Deep learning \and Financial NLP \and FinBERT
\and Gated attention \and Stochastic Weight Averaging \and FinMMEval.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
Predicting the short-term direction of financial assets is a canonical testbed
for multimodal learning~\cite{Shakeel2026,jiang2025multi}. Market behaviour emerges from the interaction of price
dynamics, the flow of textual information, and discrete corporate events, each
differing in sampling frequency, granularity and semantics. The engineering
challenge is to design an architecture that \emph{synchronizes and weights}
these signals without allowing high-cardinality modalities to dominate sparse but
informative ones, and without forcing the model to integrate evidence that does
not exist for a given instrument on a given day.

This work centres on the \emph{architectural engineering} of multimodal fusion;
the financial task serves as a controlled validation case rather than as the
objective in itself. The same design concerns recur in any domain where
continuous sensor streams, free text and discrete events must be combined.

Our contributions are: (i) a four-branch architecture with \emph{gated fusion
and availability masking}, where each modality can be differentiably silenced
when its evidence is null or inapplicable; (ii) a \emph{per-asset
volatility-adaptive labelling} scheme that balances the class distribution
across instruments that exhibit widely different volatilities; (iii) a training protocol
that \emph{separates model optimization from decision optimization}, fixing the
activation threshold during training and tuning it only once at evaluation; (iv)
the integration of \emph{Stochastic Weight Averaging} (SWA)~\cite{izmailov2018}
as an implicit regularizer, with an ablation quantifying its effect; and (v) a documented failure mode: ensembling degrades out-of-sample
performance under short evaluation windows.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Related Work}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
Deep learning for financial forecasting has been surveyed
extensively~\cite{sezer2020}. News-driven prediction was popularized by
event-embedding models~\cite{ding2015} and attention-based frameworks that
weight news by relevance~\cite{hu2018}. The introduction of domain-adapted
language models, notably FinBERT~\cite{yang2020,araci2019}, enabled robust
sentiment and representation extraction from financial text. For the price
stream, transformer encoders~\cite{vaswani2017} have been adapted to time series;
PatchTST~\cite{nie2023} showed that segmenting a series into subseries-level
patches yields strong, parameter-efficient forecasters.

Multimodal fusion has a well-established taxonomy~\cite{baltrusaitis2019}. Gated
multimodal units~\cite{arevalo2017} learn per-modality importance weights, a
mechanism we extend with an explicit availability mask so that inapplicable
modalities (e.g.\ filings for cryptocurrencies) are excluded by construction.
On the optimization side, SWA~\cite{izmailov2018} averages weights along the
training trajectory to reach flatter optima that generalize better, and
AdamW's decoupled weight decay~\cite{loshchilov2019} stabilizes training by
applying L2 regularization independently of the adaptive per-parameter learning
rates.
For labelling, the difficulty of defining directional targets under
non-stationary volatility is discussed in the financial machine-learning
literature~\cite{lopezdeprado2018}; we adopt a per-asset percentile dead-zone
instead of a global static threshold.

\section{Problem Formulation and Data}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
For each asset on a given day $t$, the model observes three distinct sources
of evidence that collectively form the multimodal input
$\mathcal{X}_t = \{\mathbf{S}_t,\ \mathcal{N}_t,\ \mathcal{E}_t\}$. The first modality is a price-and-indicator window $\mathbf{S}_t$. This is a
matrix of size $L \times d_s$, where $L = 30$ is the look-back window in
trading days and $d_s = 19$ is the number of hand-crafted scalar
features---returns, volatilities, RSI,
MACD, etc.---where each row represents one trading day and each column one feature.
The second modality $\mathcal{N}_t$ consists of news headlines. On a day with $k_t$
headlines---where $k_t$ varies per day and can be zero---the set is denoted
$\mathcal{N}_t = \{n_t^{(1)}, \dots, n_t^{(k_t)}\}$. Each headline $n_t^{(i)}$
is a short text string that will later be embedded by a financial language
model. The third modality $\mathcal{E}_t$ captures corporate filings. We denote by $\mathcal{E}_t$ the
set of filings (10-K annual reports and 10-Q quarterly reports) that are active
on day $t$, represented through their pre-computed embeddings. Unlike news,
filings are discrete events: they appear infrequently and apply only to equity
instruments, not to cryptocurrencies. Given these inputs, the goal is a learned function
$f_\theta : \mathcal{X}_t \mapsto y_t \in
\{\textsc{sell},\textsc{hold},\textsc{buy}\}$ that produces one daily
directional recommendation per asset.

\subsection{Corpus}
The corpus is provided by the \emph{FinMMEval Task~3} framework~\cite{qian2025agents} and is built from
the publicly released \texttt{daily\_news} collection accessed from the Hugging
Face Hub (\texttt{TheFinAI/daily\_news}). It comprises twelve assets---ten US equities (AAPL, ADBE, AMZN,
BMRN, GOOGL, META, MRNA, MSFT, NVDA, TSLA) and two cryptocurrencies (BTC,
ETH)---over the period 2025-08-01 to 2026-04-25. Each daily record contains four fields: the closing price, a momentum tag, the set of news headlines for the day, and optional filings (10-K annual reports and 10-Q quarterly reports). News coverage spans all trading days for every asset; filings are available only for equities. The raw corpus contains 2{,}446 daily records in total.

Preprocessing steps---weekend consolidation, news decay weighting, feature engineering, and target construction---are described in the next section.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Methodology}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
Having defined the multimodal input space and described the raw corpus, we now
detail the pipeline that transforms raw records into trainable tensors. We begin
with data preprocessing and labeling, then present the architecture, the gated
fusion mechanism, and the training protocol.

\subsection{Data preprocessing and labeling}

\paragraph{Weekend consolidation.}
For equities we remove non-trading rows---identified as weekend days or any day
with a missing closing price---and carry their news forward to the next trading
day with an exponential temporal decay. The carry-forward weight is
$w = \exp(-\lambda\,\Delta_{\text{days}})$ where $\lambda = 0.18$ and
$\Delta_{\text{days}}$ counts the number of non-trading days between the original
and the target date. For example, Friday news moved to Monday have
$\Delta_{\text{days}} = 2$ (Saturday and Sunday), yielding
$w = \exp(-0.18 \times 2) \approx 0.70$. We do \emph{not} additionally drop
exchange holidays: the source dataset supplies one record per weekday, so each
equity retains $191$ weekday records over the period (of which a handful are
NYSE holidays carried in the source with a valid price). Cryptocurrencies trade
seven days a week and require no consolidation.

\paragraph{Feature engineering.}
A single feature-engineering module is shared between offline training and the
serving runtime, guaranteeing reproducibility. From roughly sixty candidate
indicators (returns at multiple horizons, realized volatilities, RSI, MACD,
Bollinger position, trend slopes, calendar dummies, news counts, filing-decay
features and cross-asset proxies) we select $19$ features through the combination
of Spearman correlation, mutual information, LightGBM~\cite{ke2017} gain under a
purged walk-forward cross-validation with a five-day embargo, and a variance
inflation factor filter to remove collinear variables.\footnote{The $19$
retained features are: cross-asset (\texttt{btc\_return\_1d},
\texttt{mkt\_proxy\_return\_1d}); return/trend (\texttt{return\_5d},
\texttt{price\_slope\_20d}, \texttt{ema\_26}, \texttt{dist\_from\_max},
\texttt{gap\_overnight\_proxy}); volatility (\texttt{realized\_vol\_5d},
\texttt{realized\_vol\_20d}, \texttt{vol\_of\_vol}); momentum
(\texttt{rsi\_14}, \texttt{macd\_hist}); calendar (\texttt{day\_of\_month},
\texttt{dow\_1}, \texttt{dow\_2}, \texttt{dow\_4}); news (\texttt{has\_news},
\texttt{log\_news\_count}); and filing recency (\texttt{days\_since\_10q}).}

\paragraph{Target construction.}
Let $t$ index trading days and $a \in \{1,\dots,12\}$ index assets. The label
for asset $a$ on day $t$ is based on the one-day-ahead realized return
\begin{equation}
r_{t,a} = \frac{p_{t+1,a} - p_{t,a}}{p_{t,a}},
\end{equation}
where $p_{t,a}$ is the closing price. A positive $r$ means the asset rose; a
negative $r$ means it fell.

Because volatility differs across instruments --- MSFT moves less per day than
ETH --- a fixed threshold would either over-label the volatile ones (capturing
noise as signals) or under-label the stable ones (missing genuine moves). We
therefore estimate a \emph{per-asset} dead-zone threshold $\tau_a$. The
dead-zone is the interval $[-\tau_a, +\tau_a]$ inside which the return is
deemed too small to act on (label: \textsc{hold}); outside it the direction
determines the signal.

The threshold $\tau_a$ is set to the $q$-th percentile of the absolute daily
returns $|r_{s,a}|$ observed over an initial in-sample window $W_a$ for that
asset (the first $70\%$ of its time series). Here $\mathrm{P}_q$ denotes the
$q$-th percentile function (so $\mathrm{P}_{33}$ is the 33rd percentile) and
$s$ indexes the days contained in $W_a$:
\begin{equation}
\tau_a = \mathrm{P}_{q}\big(\{|r_{s,a}| : s \in W_a\}\big),
\qquad q = 33.
\end{equation}
Using absolute returns ensures we measure \emph{magnitude} only, ignoring
direction. With $q = 33$, roughly one third of the days in $W_a$ fall above
$+\tau_a$ (label: \textsc{buy}), one third below $-\tau_a$ (label:
\textsc{sell}), and one third inside the dead-zone (label: \textsc{hold}).
Because the percentile is computed per asset, this balance holds
independently of each instrument's volatility level.

The categorical label for each day $t$ and asset $a$ is therefore
\begin{equation}
y_{t,a}=
\begin{cases}
\textsc{buy}, & r_{t,a} > +\tau_a,\\[2pt]
\textsc{sell}, & r_{t,a} < -\tau_a,\\[2pt]
\textsc{hold}, & |r_{t,a}| \le \tau_a.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Aggregated across all twelve assets, this procedure yields the near-uniform
class counts reported in Table~\ref{tab:dataset} ($524/529/535$ for
sell/hold/buy in training). The counts are close to, but not exactly, one third
each for three reasons: (i) $\tau_a$ is estimated on the $70\%$ window $W_a$ but
the reported counts are taken over the $65\%$ training rows, a slightly
different sample; (ii) each asset has its own $\tau_a$, so concatenating twelve
per-asset series does not preserve the exact per-asset proportions; and (iii)
the buy/sell split reflects a mild upward drift over the period (buy marginally
exceeds sell). The hold share stays pinned near one third by construction of the
$33$rd-percentile dead-zone.
In contrast, a static global threshold would under-represent the active class
for low-volatility instruments and over-represent noise for volatile ones.
The per-asset percentile instead produces thresholds ranging from $0.18\%$ (MSFT)
to $0.86\%$ (ETH), which track each instrument's volatility and keep the three
classes approximately balanced. Figure~\ref{fig:deadzone} illustrates this on
three assets of increasing volatility: the dead-zone widens with the
instrument's typical daily move while the \textsc{hold} share stays near one
third. Additionally, an auxiliary regression target---the return normalized by a
20-day rolling realized volatility, lagged one day to avoid leakage---supervises
a secondary head.

\paragraph{Temporal splits and tensorization.}
Each trading day of each asset becomes one training sample. A sample bundles
(i) the $30$-day trailing window $\mathbf{S}_t$ of scalar features
(left-padded by repeating the first available day when fewer than $30$ days
precede $t$, so no future information is used), (ii) the day's news set
$\mathcal{N}_t$, and (iii) the current filing state $\mathcal{E}_t$. Because the
window is trailing rather than a strided slice, an asset with $n$ trading days
yields $n$ samples (minus its final day, which has no next-day return). This
gives $124/28/39$ train/validation/test samples per equity and $174/40/53$ per
cryptocurrency; summed over the ten equities and two cryptocurrencies (the two
crypto series each lose their last day) the totals are $1{,}588/360/496$.

Splitting is performed \emph{per asset} on each asset's own chronological
timeline, and the resulting samples are pooled into shared train/validation/test
sets. Samples are mutually independent---each uses only its own asset's past
window---so pooling them and shuffling the training loader introduces no
cross-asset look-ahead: an asset's test window always follows its own train and
validation windows in time. The per-asset split boundaries also align in
calendar time, since $191$ equity weekdays span essentially the same interval as
$267$ crypto calendar days; the reported \emph{unique trading dates} ($40$
validation, $53$ test) are therefore driven by the crypto daily grid, with
equity weekdays forming a subset. Weekends contribute only the two crypto assets
while weekdays contribute up to twelve, giving on average $\approx 9$ active
assets per validation date ($360/40$).

\paragraph{On the estimation window and look-ahead.}
The window $W_a$ (first $70\%$) is slightly wider than the $65\%$ training split,
so it overlaps the first few validation days; it never reaches the test
partition. This is deliberate and has no measurable effect on the reported
results, for two reasons. First, $\tau_a$ is a single per-asset \emph{magnitude}
derived from $|r|$ (no directional or future information) and, empirically,
recomputing it on the strict $65\%$ training split leaves \emph{all} $360$
validation labels unchanged and alters only $2$ of $496$ test labels ($0.4\%$);
model selection, which is performed on validation, is therefore invariant.
Second, the headline financial metrics (CR, SR, MD, DV, AV) are computed from
realized returns and the model's discrete signals and are \emph{independent of}
$\tau_a$, which enters only the auxiliary classification metrics (accuracy and
macro-F1). The reported test performance is thus free of look-ahead.

\label{sec:methodology-arch}

$d$-dimensional outputs are integrated by a gated fusion layer. Identity
information---an 8-dimensional learned symbol embedding concatenated with an
asset-type flag---is appended before the output heads, so the network always
knows which instrument it is scoring.

\paragraph{Branches.}
Table~\ref{tab:branches} summarises the four modality branches; all output
vectors of dimension $d = 32$, which are then integrated by the gated fusion
layer. A scalar MLP (Multi-Layer Perceptron) encodes the day-$t$ feature
vector. A compact PatchTST~\cite{nie2023} encoder segments the 30-day window
into non-overlapping patches of length five and applies a two-layer transformer
with mean pooling. News are embedded with a frozen FinBERT
(\texttt{finbert-tone})~\cite{yang2020}; the day's headlines are aggregated by
an attention pooling whose query is the concatenation of the symbol embedding
and the asset-type flag, with attention logits additively biased by the
temporal decay weight. A filing projection encodes the running filing embedding
after applying a 45-day half-life decay.

\paragraph{Branch details.}
For completeness we specify each branch; all project to $d = 32$ and use dropout
$0.35$ and GELU activations. \emph{(1) Scalar MLP:}
$\mathrm{Linear}(19{\to}128) \to \mathrm{GELU} \to \mathrm{Dropout} \to
\mathrm{Linear}(128{\to}32) \to \mathrm{LayerNorm}$. \emph{(2) PatchTST:} the
$30$-day window is cut into six non-overlapping patches of length five, each
flattened over the $19$ features and projected by
$\mathrm{Linear}(95{\to}32)$; learned positional embeddings are added and two
transformer blocks ($4$ heads, feed-forward $32{\to}64{\to}32$) are applied,
followed by mean pooling over patches. The internal model width equals the
fusion width ($32$). \emph{(3) News attention:} each headline is encoded by the
frozen FinBERT \texttt{[CLS]} vector ($768$-d); keys and values are
$\mathrm{Linear}(768{\to}32)$ and the query is $\mathrm{Linear}(9{\to}32)$ of
the identity vector (8-d symbol embedding $\oplus$ asset-type flag). The
attention score for headline $i$ is
$s_i = (\mathbf{q}\!\cdot\!\mathbf{k}_i)/\sqrt{32} + \log w_i$, i.e.\ the temporal
decay weight enters \emph{additively in log-space} (so $w_i\!\to\!0$ suppresses a
headline); a softmax over the $\le 8$ headlines gives the pooled vector
$\sum_i \mathrm{softmax}(s)_i\,\mathbf{v}_i$, followed by
$\mathrm{Linear}(32{\to}32) + \mathrm{LayerNorm}$. \emph{(4) Filing projection:}
the running filing embedding is pre-computed by FinBERT ($768$-d; long filings
are chunked at $384$ tokens and mean-pooled over up to $12$ chunks). When a new
10-K/10-Q arrives it \emph{replaces} the running embedding and resets its age;
otherwise the stored vector is scaled element-wise by
$0.5^{\Delta/45}$ ($45$-day half-life, $\Delta$ = days since the last filing)
and then projected by
$\mathrm{Linear}(768{\to}32)\to\mathrm{GELU}\to\mathrm{Dropout}\to
\mathrm{Linear}(32{\to}32)\to\mathrm{LayerNorm}$. Because FinBERT is frozen, the
filing and news $768$-d embeddings are fixed inputs, not learned parameters.

\subsection{Gated fusion with availability masking}
Let $m \in \{1,2,3,4\}$ index the four branches (scalar MLP, PatchTST, news
attention, filing projection). For a given input, branch $m$ produces a
$d$-dimensional representation $\mathbf{h}_m \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ (here $d=32$).
Each branch also has a binary availability indicator $a_m \in \{0,1\}$ that is
$1$ when the modality is present and $0$ when it is not. Specifically,
$a_{\text{filing}} = 0$ for cryptocurrencies (which have no filings) and
$a_{\text{news}} = 0$ on days when no news headlines are available for the
asset; the other two indicators are always $1$.

We now compute one scalar gate $g_m$ per branch, which controls how much of
that modality is let through. The gate for branch $m$ should depend not only on
the branch's own content but also on what the other branches are reporting: for
instance, if news and filings are both silent, the remaining modalities should
carry more weight. To achieve this, we first concatenate all four branch
vectors into a single vector:
\[
\mathbf{h}_{\text{all}} = [\mathbf{h}_1 \;\Vert\; \mathbf{h}_2 \;\Vert\;
\mathbf{h}_3 \;\Vert\; \mathbf{h}_4]
\quad \in \mathbb{R}^{128}.
\]
Since each $\mathbf{h}_m$ has $32$ components and there are $4$ of them, the
concatenated vector has $4 \times 32 = 128$ components---it is simply one long
vector that packs all the information together.

A learned matrix $W_g$ then reads this $128$-dimensional vector and produces
four numbers, one per branch:
\[
W_g \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 128},
\qquad
W_g\,\mathbf{h}_{\text{all}} \in \mathbb{R}^{4}.
\]
Concretely, $W_g$ has $4$ rows; the $m$-th row is a $128$-dimensional vector
of weights. When multiplied by $\mathbf{h}_{\text{all}}$, the $m$-th row
produces a scalar logit that can draw on all $128$ components---and therefore
on every branch---to decide how much to let branch $m$ through:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g} = \sigma\!\big(W_g\,\mathbf{h}_{\text{all}}\big),
\qquad
g_m = \mathbf{g}_m \cdot a_m .
\end{equation}
Sigmoid $\sigma$ keeps each gate in $(0,1)$. The availability mask $a_m$
zeros out the gate entirely when the modality is absent, so the network never
integrates non-existent evidence.

The gated representation of branch $m$ is $\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_m = g_m
\mathbf{h}_m$. These four $32$-dimensional vectors are then refined through a
lightweight cross-attention layer~\cite{vaswani2017} that treats the four
branches as tokens.
Let $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 32}$ be matrices whose rows are the
gated vectors $\tilde{\mathbf{h}}_m$, used for all three roles (each branch
attends to the others). The attention output is
\begin{equation}
A = \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^{\mathsf{T}}}{\sqrt{32}}\right) V
\quad \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 32}.
\end{equation}
The final fused representation $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{32}$ is the weighted
average of the four attention outputs, where the weight of each branch is
proportional to its gate:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z} = \sum_{m=1}^{4} \frac{g_m}{\sum_{j} g_j} \, A_{m,:}.
\end{equation}
This gating-plus-attention design lets the network dynamically suppress
missing or uninformative modalities while allowing the relevant ones to
cross-influence each other before the final prediction.

\subsection{Loss, optimization and stabilization}
\label{sec:methodology-loss}
The objective combines a classification loss and a regression loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \alpha\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + (1-\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{Huber}},
\qquad \alpha = 0.8.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Classification loss.}
Let $C = 3$ be the number of classes (sell, hold, buy). For a sample with true
label $y \in \{1,\dots,C\}$ and predicted probabilities $\mathbf{p} \in
\Delta^{C-1}$ (the simplex), the class-balanced cross-entropy with label
smoothing~\cite{szegedy2016} is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} = - \sum_{c=1}^{C} w_c \; \tilde{y}_c \log p_c,
\end{equation}
where $w_c = N / (C \cdot N_c)$ balances the classes ($N$ = total samples,
$N_c$ = samples of class $c$), and $\tilde{y}_c$ is the smoothed label
distribution
\begin{equation}
\tilde{y}_c =
\begin{cases}
1 - \varepsilon + \varepsilon / C, & c = y,\\[2pt]
\varepsilon / C, & c \neq y,
\end{cases}
\qquad \varepsilon = 0.10.
\end{equation}
Label smoothing discourages the model from assigning full probability to the
training label, which acts as a regulariser.

\paragraph{Regression loss.}
The secondary head predicts the volatility-normalised return
$r^{\text{norm}}_{t,a}$ --- the one-day-ahead return divided by a
20-day rolling realised volatility, lagged one day to avoid leakage. The loss
is the Huber~\cite{huber1964} loss with $\delta = 1.0$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Huber}} =
\begin{cases}
\frac{1}{2} (r^{\text{pred}} - r^{\text{norm}})^2,
& |r^{\text{pred}} - r^{\text{norm}}| \leq \delta,\\[4pt]
\delta \, |r^{\text{pred}} - r^{\text{norm}}| - \frac{1}{2}\delta^2,
& \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Huber loss behaves quadratically for small residuals and linearly for large
ones, making it less sensitive to outliers than mean squared error. The
regression head is an \emph{auxiliary} multi-task signal: it shares the fused
representation $\mathbf{z}$ with the classifier (the two heads differ only in
their final linear layers) and provides a denser, magnitude-aware gradient that
regularises the shared trunk. It is used only during training---at inference the
trading signal is produced solely by the classification head, so $\tau_a$ and
the regression target never enter the reported financial metrics. The weight
$\alpha = 0.8$ was fixed a priori to keep classification dominant; a
hyperparameter search additionally explored $\alpha \in [0.55, 0.95]$ without a
consistent improvement, so the default was retained.

\paragraph{Optimizer and schedule.}
We optimise the total loss with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019}, which decouples
weight decay from the adaptive gradient updates:
\begin{equation}
\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta_t \frac{\hat{m}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \epsilon}
- \eta_t \lambda \theta_t,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{m}_t$ and $\hat{v}_t$ are bias-corrected first and second moments,
$\eta_t$ is the learning rate, $\lambda$ the weight decay coefficient, and
$\epsilon$ a small constant. The learning rate follows a five-epoch linear
warmup followed by a cosine decay to zero over the remaining epochs. We clip
gradients to a maximum norm of $1.0$ to prevent explosive updates. Three
temporal augmentations are applied during training: Gaussian noise ($\sigma =
0.03$) added to the scalar features, random dropout of news headlines
(probability $0.30$), and a random time shift of $\pm 2$ days applied to the
price window.

\paragraph{Decoupling decision from optimization.}
Converting class probabilities into a discrete action requires an activation
threshold $\theta$. Optimizing $\theta$ jointly with the weights---selecting,
each epoch, the value that maximizes a financial metric on validation---induces
overfitting when the validation set has \emph{few unique trading dates} (here,
40). The annualized Sharpe ratio over so few days has large sampling variance,
and a fine threshold sweep exploits accidents of the particular realization.
We therefore (i) hold $\theta=0.50$ \emph{fixed} during training and select the
model by a combined score $0.5\,\tanh(\mathrm{SR}/3)+0.5\,\mathrm{F1}$ that is
robust to the threshold, and (ii) tune $\theta$ \emph{once} on validation after
training, by grid search in $[0.34,0.70]$, transferring the chosen value to the
test set.

\paragraph{Stochastic Weight Averaging.}
SWA~\cite{izmailov2018} averages the weights visited in the final training phase,
$\bar{\theta}_{\text{SWA}}=\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k} \theta_k$, approximating the
centre of a flat region of the loss surface. Flat minima are less sensitive to
distribution shift between validation and test, which is the dominant failure
mode in this low-data regime. We start averaging at epoch~30 with a reduced
constant learning rate and recompute normalization statistics with one
post-training pass over the training set. The averaged checkpoint is evaluated
as an independent model.

\subsection{Main results}
We compare four checkpoints, all sharing the architecture of
Section~\ref{sec:methodology-arch} and the same corpus and splits. The
\emph{prior baseline} is an earlier checkpoint of the identical architecture
($d{=}32$, two PatchTST layers, dropout $0.35$) trained under our previous
protocol, which selected the model on a noisy simulated Sharpe and tuned the
decision threshold \emph{during} training---the practice this paper argues
against (Section~\ref{sec:methodology-loss}). The \emph{base} and \emph{proposed} models use the present
protocol (fixed training threshold, validation-only threshold tuning); ``base''
is the non-averaged checkpoint and ``proposed'' applies SWA to it. We stress that
``proposed'' denotes the \emph{validation-selected operating point} of our
protocol, not a method claimed to be universally superior; the across-seed analysis (Section~\ref{sec:seed}) qualifies this
directly.

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports test-set performance of the validation-selected

\subsection{Ablation: effect of SWA}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} isolates SWA on the deployed (validation-selected) seed.
For this checkpoint, averaging the final-phase weights improves the Sharpe ratio
from $2.88$ to $4.21$ and reduces the maximum drawdown from $5.2\%$ to $4.5\%$.
We caution against over-reading this within-seed gain: as shown in
Section~\ref{sec:seed}, SWA helped three of five seeds and hurt two, and did not
improve the across-seed mean. The honest reading is therefore that SWA yielded
the single best validation-selected model in our runs, not that it uniformly
improves generalization on this corpus. We also note that the SWA model attains
a lower macro-F1 ($0.27$ vs.\ $0.40$): at the higher tuned threshold it trades
sparsely and selectively, raising risk-adjusted return while lowering nominal
classification agreement---a reminder that accuracy and risk-adjusted profit are
distinct objectives.

\subsection{Seed robustness}
\label{sec:seed}
Because the validation set spans few unique dates, single-seed test estimates
carry substantial variance. Table~\ref{tab:seed} reports the distribution over
five seeds of the deployed configuration; in every case the operating
checkpoint is chosen by the validation criterion before being measured on test.
The variance is large for both variants (test-SR standard deviation $\approx
1.8$--$2.0$), and three observations follow. First, the headline figure
(SR~$4.21$) corresponds to the seed with the highest validation Sharpe ($5.54$),
i.e.\ it is the \emph{validation-selected operating point}, not an expected
value. Second, SWA improved three of the five seeds but degraded the other two,
so it does \emph{not} raise the across-seed mean in this regime; its concrete
benefit here is to produce the single best validation-selected checkpoint.
Third, the wide spread is itself the central empirical message: with $53$ test
dates, point estimates of risk-adjusted return are statistically fragile and
must be read as such.

\subsection{Negative result: ensembling}
Contrary to the usual variance-reduction expectation, both a five-seed ensemble
and a top-$k$ configuration ensemble \emph{degraded} out-of-sample performance.
The five-seed probability-averaging ensemble of the deployed configuration
reached a test Sharpe of $+0.81$ (CR $+2.9\%$), far below the
validation-selected single model and below the base across-seed mean. Averaging
weights systematically wrong members equally with good ones; given the large
seed variance documented in Section~\ref{sec:seed}, a subset of members
collapses toward the majority \textsc{hold} prior and the average inherits that
collapse. We conclude that, with a validation set of low temporal cardinality,
member filtering is a prerequisite for ensembling, and we adopt a
single-model-with-SWA strategy instead.

\subsection{Training dynamics and confusion structure}
Figure~\ref{fig:training} shows that the training loss decreases smoothly while
the validation Sharpe (at fixed threshold) is highly volatile---direct evidence
for the small-sample argument motivating our threshold policy. Validation
macro-F1 is comparatively stable, which is why it anchors the selection score.
Figure~\ref{fig:confusion} shows the test confusion matrix of the SWA model;
the high tuned threshold concentrates predictions on \textsc{hold}, with the
active classes used selectively.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
The improvements stem from pipeline-level engineering---volatility-adaptive
labelling, the separation of model and decision optimization, and SWA---rather
than from radical architectural novelty, suggesting that in multimodal problems
with limited data the binding constraint is often the experimental protocol
rather than model capacity.

Several limitations must be stated explicitly. \emph{(i) Short evaluation
horizon.} The test set spans only $53$ unique trading dates ($\approx$2.5
months); an annualized Sharpe obtained by scaling a short-window mean-to-standard
deviation ratio by $\sqrt{252}$ has wide confidence intervals, and we did not
compute bootstrap intervals. The headline figure should therefore be read as
indicative, not definitive. \emph{(ii) Small validation set.} With $40$ unique
dates, the single threshold tuned on validation is itself a high-variance
estimate. \emph{(iii) Seed sensitivity.} Performance varies strongly across seeds
(test-SR standard deviation $\approx 1.8$--$2.0$); the deployed checkpoint was
chosen by validation score, but the across-seed mean is far below the deployed
value and SWA does not improve that mean (Table~\ref{tab:seed}). The
contribution of this paper is therefore better stated as a study of protocol
under data scarcity than as a claim of a robustly superior model. \emph{(iv) No transaction
costs or slippage} are modelled, and the equal-weight active-position portfolio
is a simplification. \emph{(v) Data-source provenance.} The competition-venue
metadata could not be independently verified and is reported as provided.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
We presented a gated multimodal architecture that fuses price series, FinBERT
news embeddings and corporate filings through availability-masked gating, and we
validated it on the FinMMEval Task~3 corpus~\cite{qian2025agents}. The validation-selected model,
combining per-asset adaptive labelling, decoupled threshold optimization and
Stochastic Weight Averaging, achieved a test cumulative return of $23.3\%$, a
Sharpe ratio of $4.21$ and a maximum drawdown of $4.45\%$. We reported these as
validation-selected operating points and showed, through a seed study, that the
across-seed distribution is wide and that neither SWA nor ensembling robustly
improves the mean in this short-horizon regime---results we present as a
methodological caution rather than as a universally superior model. Future work
includes bootstrap confidence intervals over a longer test horizon, filtered
ensembling, instrument-aware calibration, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning of
the text encoder.
